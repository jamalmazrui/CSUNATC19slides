HTML email is a subset of our beloved modern-day HTML of the web

Born in an era when CSS was just a baby and layout tables were king, coding HTML email can feel like a step back in time.

◦ Litmus, an email testing service, advertises 90+ email clients commonly used today.

◦ This huge variety of email clients and capabilities forces us to stick with a lot of older techniques and workarounds.

In-line markup is the norm, forcing developers to jumble styles with content way more than we'd like.

Still in need of universal standardization

Despite its shortcomings, HTML is the most accessible medium for email

The workarounds are worth it. Our customers experiences matter more than our professional desire for perfectly efficient, elegant markup.

◦ ARIA can be helpful, at times, and we don’t have to worry about JavaScript!

HTML email provides additional formatting and semantics that simply aren't available with plaintext, and intelligent design can help us achieve our perceivable, operable, and understandable principles.

Achieving robustness might seem impossible without universal standards, but automated brute force testing can help.

Mobile First

Your audience is likely to read your email on a mobile device

Mobile first development encourages us to simplify the layout of our emails and focus on what’s important – the message we are trying to deliver

◦ When we first started developing email content with a mobile audience in mind, we often ended up coding two versions of the email. Over time, we realized that the mobile version looked great on desktop, too, and adopted a simpler, single column layout.

Mobile email clients provide better support for embedded style sheets and media queries. Desktop styling is provided inline and then overridden when the media query is active.

◦ <style type="text/css">

@media only screen and (max-width: 480px) { <!-- mobile styles --> } </style>



Layout Tables

Yuck! We’re not fans either, but they are often a fact of life in the email development world

We can make them accessible to keyboard and screen reader users

Maintain a logical reading order

◦ Tables are read by row, from left to right. Don’t let sidebar content get mixed in to the main body of your message

◦ A single column layout is the easiest

Hide the table semantics from assistive technology by using a little ARIA

◦ <table role="presentation">

◦ Not needed on table child elements, such as <tr>, <th>, <td>

◦ Is needed on every layout <table> element, even nested ones

Alternative Text

Text alternatives are critical for users who cannot see. Users may also block images by default, either for security, privacy, or to reduce data consumption

Provide alt text for all meaningful images

◦ Images that contain text should have matching alt text

<img src="facebook-like.jpg" alt="Like us on Facebook">

Decorative images require an empty alt attribute

◦ <img src="footer-swoosh.jpg" alt="" />

Color Contrast (1 of 2)

Ensure text and background color combinations provide adequate contrast. At a minimum, regular text should provide a 4.5:1 contrast ratio. For large text and user interface graphics, the minimum ratio is reduced to 3:1

◦ Users are likely to view your email on a mobile device with a smaller screen, possibly in bright sunlight. You may want to use 4.5:1 as the minimum for all text

◦ Don’t forget to check the contrast ratio on images of text

◦ “User interface graphics” includes icons, the underline below links, and the background or border around a call to action button

Color Contrast (2 of 2)

Some email clients (Gmail, iOS) may create links for text that appears to be an address, phone number or date. This functionality can be great for users, but you have to ensure it doesn’t conflict with the background color or image.

Structure and Semantics

Provide headings

◦ A short, straightforward email may not benefit much from headings, but anything with multiple sections of content will

◦ Use semantic heading elements (<h1>, <h2>, etc.)

◦ Some email clients will apply their own styling, especially margins, so it’s a good idea to specify your own

◦ Helps ensure that even if styles are ignored by the email client, the email will maintain the visual hierarchy

Prefer semantic HTML

◦ <h1-6>, <p>, <ul>, <ol>, <li>

HTML emails are HTML documents

◦ Set the document language

<html lang="en-US">

◦ Set a document title using the email subject

<title>Contact Information Changed</title>

Testing Emails

It’s an HTML document - test in your browser

◦ Can easily run popular automated tools such as aXe

◦ Verify the presentation at different breakpoints

Check color contrast

◦ There are a variety of websites and tools that make it easy to get the contrast ratio for two color values. We also like to use TPG’s Colour Contrast Analyser to spot check color combinations using the eyedropper.

Use a keyboard to verify focus order

Use a screen reader to verify the reading order and ensure content is understandable ◦ VoiceOver on iOS is a good combination to use

Test in a variety of email clients to verify visual presentation

◦ There are services that can help make this testing easier

Final Thoughts

First impressions are important, and email may be the first your customer hears of you.

Email is a vital part of the communication chain for many companies. Nelnet may send up to 300,000 emails in a single day.

Accessibility needs to be a priority,  just as it is for your home page.

You may feel like you are working with ancient HTML, but you can make it accessible!
