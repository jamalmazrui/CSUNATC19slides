ADA LITIGATION Update

Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

•     At least 2,258 website accessibility cases filed in 2018

•     Court rulings still depend largely on court/judge

•     Utility of decisions limited because most on early dispositive motions

•     14 cases pro-defendant on motions to dismiss

•     21 cases pro-plaintiff (i.e., case moves forward or judgment for plaintiff)

•     Only 9th and 11th Cir. Court of Appeals have decided website accessibility lawsuits

•     Courts say telephonic access may be an appropriate alternative to an accessible website, but issue cannot be decided on motion to dismiss

•     Due process and primary jurisdiction defenses rejected by all courts

Title III 2018-19 Litigation Landscape

Robles v. Dominos Pizza LLC (9th Cir.)

•    Domino’s moved to dismiss on the due process and primary jurisdiction grounds, claiming it was unfair to hold it liable in the absence of specific regulations from the DOJ.

•    District Court agreed and dismissed lawsuit.  Robles appealed.

•    Ninth Circuit reversed & remanded. 

•     The ADA applies to websites & mobile apps.

•     Domino’s had notice of the general requirements under ADA Title III.

11th Circuit Decisions

Haynes v. Hooters of America, LLC

•     Case dismissed by district court based on prior settlement with another plaintiff; reversed by 11th Circuit

•     Hooters was only in process of making website accessible so case was not moot

•     Relief asked for by Plaintiff not covered by agreement, including request for order directing hooters to “continually update and maintain” website in accessible manner

•     Plaintiff was not a party to prior agreement and could not enforce it

•     Settling web accessibility suit with one party might not protect you from future lawsuits—only making website accessible will

Haynes v. Dunkin' Donuts, LLC

•       Reversed district court’s dismissal based on finding plaintiff had not demonstrated a nexus between website and stores.  11th Cir. found Plaintiff sufficiently alleged that the barriers on the website prevented him from accessing the services available in a physical store.

Sierra v. City of Hallandale Beach, Florida

•       Reversed dismissal; plaintiff was not required to exhaust remedies under CVAA before bringing an ADA suit.

•       If both CVAA and ADA apply, plaintiffs can pick which law and complaint process to follow

Employment Discrimination Cases

•     ADA Title I prohibits discrimination against employees with disabilities.

•     Bartleson v. Miami-Dade County School District

•     Lawsuit brought by National Federation of the Blind on behalf of a blind educator who was unable to use internal school district websites and forms.

•     Case settled, with M-DCSD agreeing to bring its digital resources into compliance with WCAG 2.0 and to pay $250,000 to plaintiff and her attorneys.

•     Spate of lawsuits in 2018 alleging violation of Title I based on allegedly inaccessible online job applications.

What Statutes May Require Accessible Websites

Statutory Background: Refresher (2)

•    The ADA requires public accommodations to ensure equal access for individuals with disabilities.

•    The ADA requires provision of “auxiliary aids and services” to ensure effective communication with individuals with disabilities, subject to undue burden and fundamental alteration exceptions.  42 U.S.C. Section 12181(b)(2)(A)(ii-iv).

•    Regulation’s list of “auxiliary aids and services” that may have to be provided includes “accessible electronic information technology.”

•    The ADA departs from other antidiscrimination statutes by requiring affirmative steps to ensure access by persons with disabilities.

Remedies

DOJ Regulatory (In)Action

•     In late 2017, the DOJ withdrew rulemaking effort begun in 2010.

•     Mid-2018, DOJ responds to congressional letter stating that ADA covers websites but says there is “flexibility” in how to comply.

•     DOJ declined invitation to weigh in on motion to dismiss in Robles v. Yum! Brands (Pizza Hut) (2018).

•     Further action from DOJ unlikely in near future.

 

 

“The Department first articulated its interpretation that the ADA applies to public accommodations’ websites over 20 years ago. This interpretation is consistent with the ADA’s title III requirement that the goods, services, privileges, or activities provided by places of public accommodation be equally accessible to people with disabilities.”

“Public accommodations have flexibility in how to comply with the ADA’s general requirements of nondiscrimination and effective communication. Accordingly, noncompliance with a voluntary technical standard for web accessibility does not necessarily indicate noncompliance with
the ADA.”

 

– Stephen E. Boyd,
Assistant Attorney General

DOT v. Scandinavian Airline System

•      Department of Transportation regulations under the ACAA required airlines to bring their websites into compliance with WCAG 2.0 Level AA by 2016.

•      Rather than make its website accessible, SAS built a secondary “assistive” site that met WCAG 2.0 guidelines.

•      DOT brought an action against SAS, challenging the use of a second website.

•      SAS ultimately agreed to make its primary website accessible and to pay a fine of up to $200,000.

•      Separate but equal prohibited by ACAA (and not a good idea regardless)

•      Separate is never equal

•      Increased costs from maintaining multiple sites

•      Forgotten “separate” sites fall out of accessibility

•      Good design is accessible

•      Don’t believe a firm that is selling you an alternative site or plug-in when they say it will make your website compliant. It won’t.

Managing Risk

Accessibility Matters

Nearly 27% of American adults have a disability that inhibits their daily function

•    The definition of “disability” is broader than most people think

•    Accessible websites are easier to use for people who are sub-literate, English language learners, or have ADHD

•    Baby boomers are currently between 52-70 years old, and will need accessible technology as some begin to lose vision, hearing, or mobility as they age

Accessibility For All will minimize legal risk

A Graying Globe

•      The number of people worldwide aged 80 and over will quadruple to 400 million by 2050

•      For the first time in history, there will be more people over the age of 65 than under the age of 14

Accessibility Takes Time… And Planning

•     Contact experienced legal counsel to discuss your risks and/or proactive solutions to become accessible

•     Secure reputable third-party technical expertise to help guide you through the process.  Experienced counsel can help you efficiently choose the right expert & apply privilege.

•     Look at your support and documentation process to make sure you hear issues that come up.

•     Test and monitor your website to determine and maintain the baseline level of compliance

•     Conformance standard is WCAG 2.0 AA

•     Assume web and mobile assets covered

•     Post accessibility statement, adopt policies, procedures, guidelines

•     Include accessibility in procurement processes

•     TRAIN!! Customer service reps, anyone who touches code or content, company-wide for cultural adoption.

Being proactive now can save you down the line
