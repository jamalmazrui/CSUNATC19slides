About the AEM Center

Our mission is to build the capacity of stakeholders to increase the availability and use of high- quality accessible materials and technologies that support improved learning opportunities and outcomes for learners with disabilities. Our stakeholders include:

●        States and districts

●        Higher ed institutions

●        Workforce development agencies

●        Publishers and ed tech developers

●        Families

Session Goal

At the end of this session, you’ll be inspired to practice at least one new accessibility skill the next time you create a material for your STEM course.

Beginning with the Why

The Opportunity

●        STEM jobs are expected to grow by almost 9% between 2014-2024 (U.S. Department of Commerce)

●        Workers in STEM jobs earned 29% more than their non-STEM counterparts in 2015 (U.S. Department of Commerce)

●        2.5 million STEM job openings were expected to go unfilled in 2018 (U.S. Department of Labor)

●        References

o   STEM Occupations: Past, Present, and Future (Bureau of Labor Statistics)

o   7 Facts about the STEM Workforce (Pew Research Center)

The Reality for Learners and Workers with Disabilities

●        Undergraduates: 28% of undergrads with 1 or more disabilities were enrolled in S&E, the same proportion as those without disabilities (2016)

●        Less likely to be enrolled full time for a full year at one institution

●        Graduates: 7% of students in S&E reported a disability (2012)

●        Employment: Approximately 1 in 10 scientists and engineers has a disability (2017)

●        Reference

o   National Science Foundation, National Center for Science and Engineering Statistics. 2017. Women, Minorities, and Persons with Disabilities in Science and Engineering: 2017. Special Report NSF 17-310. Arlington, VA.

POUR on the Accessibility

Definition of Accessibility

Definition of accessibility from U.S. Department of Education/Office for Civil Rights: A person with a disability can acquire the same information, engage in the same interactions, and enjoy the same services as a person without a disability, in an equally effective, integrated manner, and with substantially equivalent ease of use. See Joint “Dear Colleague” Letter: Electronic Book Readers

POUR: A Tool for Accessibility Best Practices

●        POUR stands for the four principles that represent an accessible experience with learning materials:

o   Perceivable

o   Operable

o   Understandable

o   Robust

o   See the AEM Center’s Designing for Accessibility with POUR

Scenario: Images

●        Content: Chemistry

●        Format: Image

●        Scenario: Instructor inserts a graph in a lecture slideshow, which is provided ahead of class for students to review.

o   No accessibility: image is inserted on a slide with no consideration for students who may not be able to see it on their screens.

o   Terrible accessibility: alt text is automatically generated (e.g., “screenshot of a cell phone”)

o   Bad accessibility: alt text is accurate but not sufficient (e.g., “a line graph”)

o   POUR on the accessibility: image description follows best practices for complex image description:

▪          A line graph shows the solubility of sodium sulfate in grams per 100 grams of water vs. temperature in degrees Celsius. The x-axis ranges from 0 to 200 degrees Celsius. The y-axis ranges from 0 to 60 grams. There are 21 points plotted on the line, which inclines to approximate coordinates 30,49 then declines to approximate coordinates 123,42 and then inclines to approximate coordinates 200,44. Select approximate coordinates are: (0,5)(20,19)(34,30)(30,49)(70,44)(123,42)(150,42.5)(200,44).

●        Resources:

o   DIAGRAM Center’s Image Description Guidelines

o   W3C’s An alt Decision Tree

Scenario: Documents

●        Content: Algebra

●        Format: Document

●        Scenario: Instructor assigns a handout with problems to be solved: quadratic equations with square roots.

o   No accessibility: print document with math expressions is handed out in class

o   Some accessibility: image inserted into digital document with alt text (e.g., “Problem five reads x squared plus 8 equals 28”)

▪          See Handout for Spoken Mathematics

o   POUR on the accessibility: MathML used to present expression as structured text. MathML is not always rendered consistently across browsers –  MathJax is a library that provides cross-browser compatibility.

●        Resources

o   AEM Center’s Creating Accessible Math

o   AEM Center’s Accessible Math Best Practices for Educators

o   AEM Center’s Accessible Documents

Scenario: Video

●        Content: Physics

●        Format: Video

●        Scenario: Instructor video records a lecture and uploads it to the schools’ YouTube channel for students’ use.

o   No accessibility: video posted without captions

o   Video with bad accessibility: video posted with automatic captions

o   POUR on the accessibility: Edit the automatic captions in YouTube, use CADET to create or edit captions

Audio Description

●        Narrator describes relevant details about the onscreen action
