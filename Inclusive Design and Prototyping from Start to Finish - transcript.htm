Slide 2

This year, while I do want to talk about Inclusive Design, I also want to talk about cooking and how when we invite people into our homes, we can best set a place for them at the table.  For me, cooking is a passion. I’m able to express myself in a number of different ways. From choosing recipes, planning menus, to hosting groups of friends for dinner, there are many parallels between what we need to do to deliver a wonderful experience to our guests and the design process needed to creative inclusive, ability-focused user experiences.

 

My hope is that you will come away with new points of connection between how to care for the people who you welcome into your home and how you design software in order that in both cases you are taking steps to set a place for them at the table where they can partake in the things that you have created, be nourished by them and be inspired to do the same thing.

 

Slide 3

To do this, we must start with hospitality. Better Crocker’s Guide to Easy Entertaining notes that the dictionary defines hospitality as “being disposed to entertain with generous kindness.” It then sets out to teach the reader…

•      How to be “disposed” by being ready in detail and in spirit.

•      How to “entertain” yourself as well as others.

•      How to be “generous” by providing delicious and suitable food.

•      How to serve it “with kindness” so that guests are not dismayed by a do-it-herself hostess who deserts them for her kitchen.

 

Now, the book was published in 1959 so some of the language is little dated.

 

Slide 4

Chefs are inclusive designers.

 

I don’t know about you, but I have a group of friends with a variety of foods allergies and preferences. Some are vegan. Some are gluten free. One just doesn’t like berries and fresh cuts tomatoes. Another friend is allergic to anything in the nightshade family. Honestly, I have to look up the list of what is included in the nightshade family every time I’m cooking for this friend. Additional considerations include age, mobility, and even the ability to chew certain foods, whether you are young or old.

 

A part of knowing how to set a place for someone at your table is to get to know their needs and preferences.

Next, we must decide who can attend. Who’s in and who’s out? The size of one’s home usually dictates how many people we can entertain. When designing software, it is rare that we are creating a product that is intended to only be used by a small number of people, so we can’t be selective about who shows up.

 

Slide 6

Kat Holmes, Founder of Mismatch.design and Director of User Experience at Google recently published a book called Mismatch: how inclusion shapes design. In it, she explores what it really means to be inclusive.

 

Slide 7

She describes inclusion using the image of a circle, a boundary. In it are the things that are effectively included, with the word “IN” in large capital letters inside the circle. Surrounding this circle are the five instance of the word “OUT” to represent how some things are shut in and others are shut out.

 

Slide 8

She goes on to provide a definition of Inclusive Design from her time at Microsoft. Inclusive Design is a methodology that enables and draws on the full range of human diversity. Most importantly, this means including and learning from people with a range of perspectives. (I would amend that to include a range of abilities.)

 

Slide 9

She goes on to describe how we must break the original model of inclusion when inclusion results in shutting others out. In this version of the earlier image, the circle now contains as series gaps around the circumference, representing the breakdown of barriers towards inclusion.

Slide 25

Merriam-Webster defines mise en place as “a culinary process in which ingredients are prepared and organized before cooking”. “The purpose of mise en place is to allow the chef to cook in the most efficient way without having to stop. While it won’t prevent you from drying out a roast, burning a batch of cookies or deflating a soufflé, it will make you a better cook by changing the way you work though preparing a meal from inception to clean-up (Mise en Place). Pictured are set of ingredients prepared to assemble in an enchilada casserole recipe.

 

By mastering certain basics like knife skills and “mise en place”, you built up a level of confidence that so that you can take on more challenging cuisine.

 

In a similar way, designers must build up foundational skills so that we are better equipped to take on the more complex user experience challenges.

Slide 28

Incorporating Social Factors

•      A study conducted at the University of Washington called, Incorporating Social Factors in Accessible Design by Kristen Shinohara, Jacob O. Wobbrock, and Wanda Pratt was conducted to test the idea that new challenges are presented when using technology in a social context.

•      Ultimately, they found beneficial outcomes when disabled and nondisabled users were grouped together with a designer working on a solution.

 

Slide 29

During the study designers used a tool called Methods Cards like the one we see pictured here. They are intended to be used alongside users in the design process rather than in lieu of, like Personas. The scenarios were drawn from prior research where participants with disabilities revealed some version of these events in studies.

This one is titled, NON-USE. Each card has two sides and is broken up into four sections, Scenario, Consider, Design + Reflect, and Ask Users.

 

Let’s step through this card.

 

Scenario

People may choose not to use technology in some social situations because they feel uncomfortable, such as when worrying that others will notice hearing aids, or when feeling rude that a talking watch announces every hour.

 

Consider

What are social situations (i.e., a party, at work) where technology use is unappealing or inappropriate?

To what extent does discomfort in technology use depend on the type of technology, i.e., accessible options, like hearing aids vs. a smartphone?

 

Design + Reflect

How might your design be inappropriate or unappealing to use when around others.

 

Ask Users

When do you feel un/comfortable using technology?

When do you choose not to use technology?

 

A link to more information about this study and the Method cards is provided at the end of this deck.

Slide 30

 

One of the challenges Top Chef competitors must face is to take a dish normally prepared in a traditional way and deliver in a completely new way. Take for example a challenge to prepare Beef Bourguignon, a beef stew braised in red wine and beef stock, flavored with carrots, onions, garlic, and a bouquet garni. The challenge is to serve this dish as a meal you can eat on the go with your hands. Do you make into a taco? Do you wrap it in a pita? Perhaps instead, you devise a way to prepare the dish such that the diner can decide how they wish the dish to be served?

 

Slide 31

Jacob Wobbrock, who is a Professor of human-computer interaction (HCI) in the Information School at the University of Washington, where he also directs the Mobile & Accessible Design Lab. He talks about a concept called Ability-based Design.

 

Ability-based design is “A design approach in which the human abilities required to operate an interactive system are questioned, and systems are made operable by and adaptable to alternative abilities.”

 

From his point of view, “Everyone has abilities, some people have more than others. We need to design for people with abilities of all types.”

 

Slide 32

Adaptive Software

 

At Adobe, we have begun building applications like Lightroom that centralize business logic and user data in the cloud, which enables us to bring the same features to various surfaces, customizing the UI to best fit each surface and ideally each user.

•      Using Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning to build software which adapts to the abilities of the user.

•      The user interface is separated from the business logic so that new interfaces can be crafted when presented with new abilities. The software would learn how to adapt to the new interface.
