New AT model…

And from the different actors perspectives…

•     Data Producer: Now just needs to produce accessible data + use standard transaction APIs

•     Software Development: Removed

•     Accessibility Fixing + Testing: Now moved to testing Assistive Technology

But, what is something goes wrong?

•    Let’s say…

•     The UI looked precisely as it should, however, upon accessibility testing it becomes apparent that occasional pages get generated that contain A11y issues?

•     The company who owns the data and is using the service is likely responsible for ensuring its accessibility conformance. 

•     They go back to the web-page-gen service provider and are informed that due to the probabilistic generation of content in an RNN there’s no way to make such precise corrections to output code.

•    So what should they do = whose responsibility is it?

NGT1: Accessible Web Page Generator…

But, what if something goes wrong?

•    Let’s say…

•     The AT user has been consistently annoyed by being presented with legal text.

•     The AT has learnt, by experience, to filter what it believes to be unimportant legal text (accepting it) – thus making the user happier.

•     On one particular site, however, the legal text was categorized by the AT as unimportant – when in fact it was important – and the AT accepted it as being read.

•     After getting an unexpected delivery, the user makes enquires with the seller but, having accepted as “read” the terms & conditions, the user  now has lost their legal protection.

•    So what should they do = whose responsibility is it?

 

 

NGT2 - ML-based AT…

In both, responsibility lies with…

•    Well, after reading some terms and conditions, the user of the service perhaps?

•     An average seller’s website e.g. NO ONE WARRANTS OR GUARANTEES THAT THE SERVICES ARE ACCURATE, RELIABLE OR CORRECT; THAT THE SERVICES WILL MEET YOUR REQUIREMENTS;

•     An average assistive technology license e.g. Licensee hereby acknowledges that the Program may contain errors, inaccuracies and omissions. Licensee assumes any and all risks as to the results and performance of the Program, including any risk, damage, loss or harm from Licensee’s use or downloading the Program.

 

Both issues actually arise from ML being probabilistic, not deterministic…

•    Essentially these systems make decisions based on their level of certainty, for example:

•     They’re 75.8% sure that this <img tag should have an alt attribute

•     They’re 93.2% sure that the user does not like to be told about “legal”

With the impact of their decisions falling on the user…

•    How long will it take before users start to place a value on a system’s level of certainty.

•    Effectively asking “In this context of use, what is certain enough?”

Finale…

•    To me, the most impactful change might come from ML-based Assistive Technology with all its wonderful possibilities.

•    But, right now, I’m thinking ML will drive incremental change into this market – rather than “extreme” change coming all of a sudden.

•    Why?

•    I think the rate of change will come at a pace set by the level of certainty needed by the user, for each increment.

•    Ultimately driven by the user having to take responsibility for:

•     their own actions taken whilst using “the thing”; and

•     any actions “the thing” takes on their behalf.

However, I could well be wrong…

•    So, I’m now watching the markets for two things:

•     Things which might have “extreme” potential impact; and

•     Things which will cause “boiling frog syndrome” – where slight but constant proliferation of ML within a market creeps-up to becomes dangerous – just like when a frog fails to get out of slowly heating water.
