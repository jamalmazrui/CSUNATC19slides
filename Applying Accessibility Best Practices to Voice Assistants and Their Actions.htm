Virtual Assistants

•     Voice assistants, and text chat bots

•     Conversational, computer-generated personas that simulate a conversation to provide contextual voice- or text-based information to users

•     Personal assistants, customer assistants, business assistants, etc.

•     Common implementations: web, software, kiosk, car, speaker, glasses, IVR, or mobile device

•     Examples

•      Google, Cortana, Alexa, Siri, and Bixby

Devices and Interaction

•    E.g. smart speakers Amazon Echo and Echo Show, Google Home, Apple HomePod

•    Virtual Assistants use natural-language processing with dialogue control and domain knowledge (using AI) to provide content within context of the dialogue.

•    The primary interaction methods are text-to-text, text-to-speech, speech-to-text and speech-to-speech.

•     Also may include image taking and visual displays

Benefits of Voice Assistants

•    Combine AI to make finding or purchasing information simpler

•    Control environment through connections w/ web of things

•    Perform tasks such as add calendar appointments and emails

•    Provide reminders (location and time), contextual help

•    Integrate with 3rd party providers – e.g. your bank, etc.

•    The future of how we may access the Internet

 

to Users with Disabilities

Benefits Cont.

•    Tend to be good at giving a single or small number of responses

•    Allow voice input -don’t have to type if you are not able to or find it easier not to (but you can type if need to)

•     Don’t require strong literacy skills

•     Features that make them accessible to some, pose challenges for others

•    Access from a distance

•    May provide conversation/companionship/
emergency contact

•     Benefits to older people great

•    Allow shared experiences with a group or family

•    Often support combining multiple actions into
one request – automate routines

 

Challenges of Voice Assistants

•     Predominant reliance on speech & audio

•     Affects those with speech impairment and those who are Deaf or hard of Hearing

•     According to the NIH In the US,

•     7.5 million people have trouble using their voice

•     3 million+ people stutter.

•     37.5M US adults report trouble hearing (NIH)

•     10M hard of hearing (within normal conversation)

•     !M functionally Deaf (SIPP)

•     5% of school aid children have auditory processing Impairments (NIH)

to Users with Disabilities

Challenges Cont.

•    May require remembering some key utterances and trigger words

•    Not as good in communal, noisy, or public environments.

•    Not as good with helping user compare vast amounts of information

•    A single mode of interaction (speech to speech) is not practical for all

•     Assistants must support voice and text and other visual forms of communication (e.g. signing).

•    Typing and text may not be equivalent experience in many situations

•     When your hands are dirty or when you can’t reach a keyboard or see the screen

Standards and Guidelines

•    Functional requirements by user

•     Cognitive and learning, deaf and hard of hearing, blind and low vision, speech impaired, motor impairment, etc.

•    WCAG 2.x is a good base

•     (Most related to cognitive)

•     Take into account WCAG to non-web ICT

•    Great design guidelines from providers

•     E.g. Alexa Skills Design Guide

Voice Interfaces

Use Clear Language

•    SC 3.1.3 Unusual Words

•     Avoid unusual words, except if required for the task

•     Don’t make people learn a new language, jargon, or new rules or layers

•    SC 3.1.4 Abbreviations

•     Avoid abbreviation and acronyms

•    SC 3.1.5 Reading Level

•     Avoid jargon, server information, and special languages

•    SC 3.1.6 Pronunciation

•     Phonemes in SSML can be used to correctly pronounce words

•    SC 3.1.1/3.1.2 Language

•     Spirit of this requirement to accurate pronunciation of TTS for language of content

 

 

 

Don’t rely on Non-text Audio

•    SC 1.2.1/1.3.3 Audio only

•     Avoid relying only on audio cues – e.g. sound for correct  answer in a game

•    Avoid using Whisper and other effects that may be difficult for some users to hear

•     Assistants use SSML to control aspects of speech including pronunciation

•    Avoid changing the speed, volume, and other factors of speech without good reason

•     SSML provides much capability but use restraint

 

Be Consistent but not Repetitive

•    SC 3.2.3 Consistent  Navigation

•     Spirit of SC is consistent but not repetitive between skills

•    SC 3.2.4 Consistent Identification between sets of skills

•     Functionality is consistently identified with utterances

 

 

 

 

 

Handling Errors

•    3.3.1 Error Identification

•     Re-prompting and continue with partial data

•    3.3.3 Error Suggestions

•     E.g. You can ask for recipes for small, medium, or large. Which did you want?

•    3.3.4 Error Prevention (Legal, Financial, Data)

•     Confirmation for high risk tasks – explicit or implicit

•     “You want to transfer $400 dollars to your savings account? Is that correct?”

•     “Ok, $400 will be transferred to your savings.”

 

Help, Instructions, and Confirmation

•    3.3.5 Help (Contextual Help)

•     A hint, re-prompt, or contextual answer

•     Me: “I’m hungry”

•     Assistant: “What would you like to order?”

•     Provide clear directions and provide sample utterances

•     Both when skill is launched (master) and in skill card

•    Provide Confirmation (landmarking)

•     Explicit - Assistant: “You would like a veggie pizza?”

•     Implicit – Assistant: “Would you like anything else with your veggie pizza”.

•     When user can lose data, confirm exit

•    Have accessible onboarding for skills that require setup through app such as authentication for banking

 

 

Adaptability & Flexibility

•    Support both none, partial, and full invocations

•     Partial commands allow people to provide information step by step,

•     Me: “I’d like to order a pizza”,

•     Assistant: “Would you like a veggie, cheese, or something else?”

•     Me: “Veggie”

•     Assistant: “Order your pizza now?”

•     Me: “yes”

•     Full commands allow people who automate commands through devices like Tecla to issue full command in one turn

•     Me: “I would like a pizza with half cheese and half olives delivered now”,

•     Assistant: “Your pizza is on it’s way”

     Note: With Alexa users have 8 seconds to respond

Adaptability & Flexibility (cont.)

•    Support short commands and alternative paths

•     E.g. timer 15 minutes” rather than “set a timer for 15 minutes”.

•    Support Name-free Interactions for users who may not recall your skill name

•     Helps users find skills which they don’t know the name

•    SC 2.4.5 Multiple ways

•     Spirit of this requirement – allow different utterances to initiate an intent

 

 

 

 

Prompts and Landmarks

•    Prompt the user (menu style and re-prompts)

•     “Chef Helper. You can ask for a recipe, the ingredients of a favorite, or cleaning tips. Now, which would you like?"”

•     Preferable over opened ended prompt “What would you like to do?

•     There are 5 choices including mac & cheese and salad, which would you like?

•     Follow-up prompts for related items

•    Allow people to repeat prompts

•    Prompt with small number of choices

•     Too many options can confuse & impact working memory

 

 

 

Handling Responses

•    Use markers

•     First, what is your zip code

•     Next, “I’ll need your street address in order to deliver your pizza”.

•    Support Repeat – allows user to request the assistant to repeat what was last said

•    SC 2.2.1 Timing Adjustable

•     Allow people to get more time – re-prompt

•    Send progressive responses when you are working to let the user know you are still processing

•     “Hang on – I am creating the game”.

 

 

 

Providing Results

•    Keep results short

•     Advise how many items are in the list and list 2 or 3 at most

•     Paginate results

•     Ordered most to least relevant

 

Requesting User Input

•    Make assumptions about data when you can to avoid users having to enter more information

•     If a request is likely about today then assume today and use that in landmarking

•     Assistant: “The weather today will be sunny”.

•     Roll with mixed correct and incorrect data

•     Make recommendations based on the data you did get

•    Support data entry or setting up authentication through other methods rather than entering long alphanumeric passwords

•    If pins are required allow people authentication methods that don't require memorization

•    Allow people multiple tries if they are not recognized
