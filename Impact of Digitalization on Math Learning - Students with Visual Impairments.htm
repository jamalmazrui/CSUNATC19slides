Digital Textbooks

 

Digital versus Traditional:

Secondary Students with Visual

Impairments’ Perceptions

of a Digital Algebra Textbook

 

Emily C. Bouck, Pei-Lin Weng, and Rajiv Satsangi

 

Journal of Visual Impairment & Blindness, January-February 2016

Findings

Ø  Demographics: Algebra 1 class of 5 students

Ø  Clear student preference for traditional textbooks

Ø  However, the digital textbook was

Ø    Easy to understand

Ø    Easy to use

Ø  Students

Ø    Wanted to learn more about technologies that would help them access algebra

Ø    Liked the Navigation aspect

Ø  Teacher and aide

Ø    Supported and appeared to encourage the students’ preferences for traditional textbooks.

Success for All

Until new technology meets ALL students’ needs, teachers must be creative in finding solutions that provide access to the same material or comparable experiences for students who are visually impaired.

Possible Solutions

Ø  Build in accessibility when designing products, websites, online tools, apps, etc.

Ø  Support of research that leads to products that can be used by students in the math classroom

Ø  Collaboration between developers of software and hardware that leads to

Ø   Multi-sensory access in mathematics

Ø   Multi-line and full page displays

Ø   Integration of braille and graphics within the same display

Professional Development

Ø  Ongoing professional development for teachers of students with visual impairment

Ø   Opportunities for teachers and students to learn together

Ø   Methods for teachers to learn any new technology quickly and easily

Ø   Design with an intuitive approach to decrease the learning curve and lessen the need for extensive professional development
