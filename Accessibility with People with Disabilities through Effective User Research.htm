User research with people with disabilities

helps you make better digital products and services

Research activities

When the objective is discovery

§   Involve people with disabilities when you want to learn more about a new design problem or opportunity:

§   Do this as early as possible in development of a new product/service or redesign of an existing product/service

§   The focus is on understanding current experiences and behavior, so you don’t need a product to evaluate

 

 

Contextual inquiry interviews

§   Spend time learning from people with disabilities about a particular context and environment

-         Go to where people are

-         A “show and tell” opportunity; including the role of technology

§   Learn about the current state

-         What do people do?

-         What works, and what doesn’t? How do people overcome issues?

§   Outcome: insights to inform and influence design decisions; uncover opportunities for innovation

When you want to evaluate something

§   Involve people with disabilities when you have a product you want to evaluate:

§   During development of a product, ideally with functional prototypes, to enable iterative improvement

§   For mature or live products, testing can help identify areas to improve or benchmark against competitors/previous versions

 

 

Usability testing

§   Ask people with disabilities to complete tasks using a product

§   Observe the participant experience

§   Capture participant reaction and explanation

§   Outcomes:

-         Location, nature and impact of barriers to success

-         Insight on where/how to focus efforts on improving designs

Customer experience testing

§   If you don’t have budget or resource for usability testing

§   Send people with disabilities a set of tasks to complete using a product

§   Participants remotely record their experience, return results to you

§   Outcomes:

-         First-hand perspectives from people with disabilities

-         Indications of where issues require further investigation

Practical tips

Deciding how many people

§   Don’t over-focus on representativeness of participants or statistically significant samples

-         Cover a range of impairments unless research focus is on one specific group

-         Don’t rely on expert AT users

§   8 is a good ballpark number for a first usability study or contextual inquiry

§   For agile user research, aim for fewer participants per evaluation and more frequent evaluations

Where to recruit

§   Possible helpful sources of participants:

-         Local disability advocacy organizations; independent living centers

-         College or university disability support centers

-         Social media

-         Word-of-mouth helps once you have a program up and running

Where to do the research

•             Go to where participants are:

-         For participants: More convenient as they don’t have to travel, more familiar

-         For researchers: May help you get more reliable data; but logistically more challenging.

•             Use a central venue:

-         For researchers: more convenient for scheduling and set-up.

-         For participants: May be difficult and expensive to travel to the venue, and potentially more stressful

A central venue

§   Look for a venue familiar and convenient to your participants, for example a community center or disability support organization.

§   Provide clear, accessible directions in advance, and a phone number should participants have trouble finding you.

§   Make sure the room set-up accommodates:

-         Wheelchair and scooter users.

-         Companions, interpreters, service animals

§   Tidy up cables, locate camera tripod legs so that they’re not a trip hazard.

Making a research activity accessible

§   Provide accessible documentation and communication

§   Provide incentives/compensation

§   Allow participants to use their own device wherever possible

-         If you need to provide the device for testing, get information in advance about AT/accessibility settings needed and set up the device accordingly

Making a research activity accessible

§   Make the resource to be evaluated as accessible as possible before the research

-         Adjust the tasks and test plan to avoid known barriers

§   Help observers understand what’s happening

-         Capture the screen and the participant

-         Mics for participant and screen reader output

-         Second monitor to show a mobile device screen

Making an impact

Useful output of user research activity

§   Stories of problems—and successes!

§   Participant quotes

§   Personas and scenarios

§   Prioritized list of issues to address

§   Performance data for comparison/benchmarking

§   Video: reusable educational material on accessibility
